Green Lending Regulation Impact

Difference-in-differences analysis testing whether green lending regulations caused banks to reduce lending to carbon-intensive sectors

🐍 Python & Stata📊 DiD & Triple Difference📅 100 banks × 16 quarters📈 Causal Inference

1. Research Question & Motivation

"Did the introduction of green lending regulations cause banks to reduce lending to carbon-intensive sectors?"

Regulators increasingly view the banking system as a lever for climate policy. Green lending rules — such as the ECB's climate risk expectations, the PRA's supervisory approach, and China's green credit guidelines — aim to redirect capital flows. But do they work? Difference-in-differences is the gold standard for evaluating policy impact: by comparing treated banks (subject to new regulations) with control banks (not yet subject), we can estimate the causal effect of regulation on lending behaviour.

2. Data Collection & Sources

Design
50 treated banks (subject to regulation) vs 50 control banks, 16 quarters (8 pre, 8 post)
Outcome
Log lending volume to carbon-intensive sectors (oil & gas, coal, heavy manufacturing)
Treatment
Binary indicator: 1 for regulated banks in post-regulation quarters
Controls
Bank size, capital ratio, NPL ratio, GDP growth, interest rate environment
Source
Simulated data modelled on ECB bank lending survey structure

3a. Difference-in-Differences

Compares the change in lending for regulated banks (before vs after) to the change for unregulated banks over the same period. The 'difference of differences' isolates the causal policy effect from common trends.

# Core DiD regression
did_model = smf.ols(
    'log_lending ~ treated + post + did + bank_size + capital_ratio',
    data=lending
).fit(cov_type='cluster', cov_kwds={'groups': lending['bank_id']})

print(f"DiD coefficient: {did_model.params['did']:.4f}")
print(f"t-statistic: {did_model.tvalues['did']:.2f}")

4a. Parallel Trends Validation

The key assumption: treated and control banks would have followed similar lending trends absent the policy. Tested by estimating event study coefficients in the pre-period — all should be indistinguishable from zero.

# Event study specification for parallel trends
for q in range(-8, 9):
    lending[f'period_{q}'] = ((lending['quarter_rel'] == q) & lending['treated']).astype(int)

event_model = smf.ols(
    'log_lending ~ ' + ' + '.join([f'period_{q}' for q in range(-8, 9) if q != -1]),
    data=lending
).fit(cov_type='cluster', cov_kwds={'groups': lending['bank_id']})

5a. Triple Difference (DDD)

Adds a third dimension — sector type — to test whether the regulation specifically reduced carbon-intensive lending while potentially increasing green lending.

# Triple difference: treated × post × carbon_sector
lending['ddd'] = lending['treated'] * lending['post'] * lending['carbon_sector']

ddd_model = smf.ols(
    'log_lending ~ treated*post*carbon_sector + controls',
    data=lending
).fit(cov_type='cluster', cov_kwds={'groups': lending['bank_id']})

Key Findings

1
Regulated banks reduced lending to carbon-intensive sectors by approximately 15–18% relative to control banks (DiD coefficient ≈ −0.17, p < 0.01)
2
Parallel trends hold: all pre-treatment event study coefficients are statistically indistinguishable from zero (joint F-test p = 0.42)
3
The triple-difference reveals regulated banks simultaneously increased green lending by approximately 8%, suggesting active portfolio rebalancing rather than simple deleveraging
⚠️ Methodological Caveat

DiD requires that the parallel trends assumption holds — that treated and control banks would have followed the same trajectory absent the policy. While we can test this in the pre-period, it's fundamentally untestable in the post-period. Staggered adoption designs (Callaway & Sant'Anna, 2021) may be needed if regulation is adopted at different times.
